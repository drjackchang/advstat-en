Topic 8：Nonparametric Tests

Mann-Whitney U, Wilcoxon Signed-Rank, Kruskal-Wallis, APA Reporting

Learning Objectives

  1. Understand the logic and appropriate use of nonparametric tests
  2. Select the correct nonparametric alternative for each situation
  3. Execute Mann-Whitney U, Wilcoxon Signed-Rank, and Kruskal-Wallis tests
  4. Produce APA-format result tables and reporting sentences

Statistical Method Map (This Topic)

X (Predictor) Y (Outcome) Parametric Method Nonparametric Alternative
Categorical (2 groups, independent) Continuous (skewed) Independent t-test Mann-Whitney U
Categorical (2 groups, paired) Continuous (skewed) Paired t-test Wilcoxon Signed-Rank
Categorical (≥ 3 groups) Continuous (skewed) One-way ANOVA Kruskal-Wallis
Continuous (skewed) Continuous (skewed) Pearson correlation Spearman (Topic 5)
Note

Nonparametric tests are not inherently inferior — they are the more honest choice when parametric assumptions are violated. In EMS research, pain scores, satisfaction ratings, and small-sample data are common candidates for nonparametric methods.

I. Statistical Theory

1.1 Why Nonparametric Tests?

Parametric tests (t-tests, ANOVA) require:

  1. Approximately normal distribution
  2. Homogeneity of variance (some methods)
  3. Continuous measurement scale

When these assumptions are seriously violated — especially with small samples (n < 30) and pronounced skewness or outliers — parametric p-values become unreliable.

Nonparametric solution: convert raw values to ranks before testing. This removes any dependence on distributional assumptions.

1.2 The Rank Transformation

Arrange all observations from smallest to largest and assign ranks:

Raw values:  3.2,  7.8,  1.1,  5.5,  9.0
Ranks:         2,    4,    1,    3,    5

Ranks are more uniformly distributed than raw data. Extreme values lose their extremity, making tests insensitive to skewness and outliers.

1.3 Mann-Whitney U Test

The nonparametric alternative to the independent t-test, for two independent samples.

H₀: The two groups have the same population distribution (or equal medians).

Logic of the U statistic: Count how many times a value from Group 1 exceeds a value from Group 2. If the distributions are identical, this count should be close to half the total number of comparisons.

\[U = n_1 n_2 + \frac{n_1(n_1+1)}{2} - R_1\]

where R₁ is the sum of ranks for Group 1.

Tip

Effect size: Use r = Z / √N, where Z is the standardized U statistic. - r ≥ .10: small effect - r ≥ .30: medium effect - r ≥ .50: large effect

1.4 Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test

The nonparametric alternative to the paired t-test, for paired or pre-post designs.

Procedure: 1. Compute difference scores: d = post − pre 2. Rank the absolute values |d| 3. Sum the ranks separately for positive and negative differences 4. The smaller sum is the W statistic

H₀: The distribution of difference scores is symmetric around zero (median difference = 0)

1.5 Kruskal-Wallis Test

The nonparametric alternative to one-way ANOVA, for three or more independent groups.

Procedure: Pool all groups, rank all observations, then compare whether mean ranks differ substantially across groups.

\[H = \frac{12}{N(N+1)} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{R_i^2}{n_i} - 3(N+1)\]

H approximates a χ² distribution with df = k − 1.

Post-hoc comparisons after significance: Dunn’s test with Bonferroni correction.

1.6 When to Use Nonparametric Tests

Check the following:
  ├── n < 30 AND Shapiro-Wilk p < .05 → nonparametric
  ├── Severe outliers that cannot be removed → nonparametric
  ├── Ordinal scale data (Likert) → nonparametric
  └── n ≥ 30 AND mild skewness → parametric remains valid
       (Central Limit Theorem provides protection)
Important

The cost of nonparametric tests: When data are truly normal, nonparametric tests have approximately 5–15% less statistical power than their parametric counterparts. They are not unconditionally better — they are more reliable when assumptions are violated.

II. Analysis Workflow

Step 1: Confirm normality violation (Shapiro-Wilk + histogram)
    ↓
Step 2: Select the appropriate nonparametric test
    ├── 2 independent groups → Mann-Whitney U
    ├── Paired / pre-post → Wilcoxon Signed-Rank
    └── ≥ 3 independent groups → Kruskal-Wallis + Dunn's test
    ↓
Step 3: Run the test and compute effect size
    ↓
Step 4: APA-format report

IV. Reporting Results (APA Format)

Mann-Whitney U:

Male EMS personnel (Mdn = 5.0) and female personnel (Mdn = 6.0) did not differ significantly in sleep quality, W = 4821, Z = −1.23, p = .219, r = .09 (small effect).

Wilcoxon Signed-Rank:

Job satisfaction was significantly higher post-intervention (Mdn = 4.0) than pre-intervention (Mdn = 3.0), V = 3241, Z = −3.45, p < .001, r = .24 (small-to-medium effect).

Kruskal-Wallis + Dunn’s test:

A Kruskal-Wallis test revealed significant differences in sleep quality across EMT certification levels, H(2) = 8.43, p = .015, ε² = .03 (small effect). Dunn’s post-hoc comparisons (Bonferroni corrected) showed that EMT-P had significantly better sleep quality than EMT-1 (p = .012); other pairwise comparisons were not significant.
